The '''Australian National University''' ('''ANU''') is a [[national university|national]] [[doctoral university|research university]] located in [[Canberra]], the capital of [[Australia]]. Its main campus in [[Acton, Australian Capital Territory|Acton]] encompasses seven teaching and research colleges, in addition to several national academies and institutes.<ref name="structure">{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/governance-structure/university-structure/academic-structure|title=Academic Structure|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

Founded in 1946, it is the only university to have been created by the [[Parliament of Australia]]. Originally a [[Postgraduate education|postgraduate]] research university, ANU commenced [[Undergraduate education|undergraduate]] teaching in 1960 when it integrated the [[Canberra University College]], which had been established in 1929 as a campus of the [[University of Melbourne]].<ref name="timeline">{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/profile/history/timeline|title=Timeline|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> ANU enrolls 10,052 undergraduate and 10,840 postgraduate students and employs 3,753 staff.<ref name="statistics">{{cite web|url=http://unistats.anu.edu.au/statistics/quickstats/|title=Quick Stats|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> The university's endowment stood at A$1.13 billion in 2012.<ref name="endowment">{{cite web|url=http://www.anu.edu.au/files/review/Investment-Flyer-2012.pdf|title=Investment Performance Summary for Year Ended 31 December 2012|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=15 March 2015}}</ref>

ANU is consistently ranked among the world's top universities. ANU is ranked co-equal 19th in the world (first in Australia) with [[King's College London]] by the 2015/16 [[QS World University Rankings]],<ref name="qs2015">{{cite web|url=http://www.topuniversities.com/university-rankings/world-university-rankings/2015#sorting=rank+region=+country=+faculty=+stars=false+search=|title=QS World University Rankings Results 2015/16|accessdate=16 September 2015|publisher=QS Quacquarelli Symonds Limited}}</ref> and 52nd in the world (second in Australia) by the 2015/16 ''[[Times Higher Education]].''<ref name=THE>{{cite web|url=https://www.timeshighereducation.com/world-university-rankings/2016/world-ranking#!/page/0/length/25/name/Australian/sort_by/rank_label/sort_order/asc/cols/rank_only|title=World University Rankings 2015–2016|accessdate=20 April 2016|publisher=''[[Times Higher Education]]''}}</ref> ANU was named the world's 25th (first in Australia) most international university in a 2016 study by ''Times Higher Education''.<ref name="IntlUni">{{cite web|url=https://www.timeshighereducation.com/features/200-most-international-universities-world-2016|title=The 25 Most International Universities|accessdate=20 April 2016|publisher=''[[Times Higher Education]]''}}</ref> In the 2015 ''Times Higher Education'' Global Employability University Ranking, an annual ranking of university graduates' employability, ANU was ranked 32nd in the world (first in Australia).<ref>{{cite web|last=Elmes |first=John |url=https://www.timeshighereducation.com/carousels/global-employability-university-ranking-2015-results|title=Times Higher Education Global Employability University Ranking 2015 &#124; News |publisher=''[[Times Higher Education]]''|date= |accessdate=20 April 2016}}</ref> ANU is ranked 89th (first in Australia) in the 2015 CWTS Leiden ranking.<ref>http://www.leidenranking.com/ranking/2015/university?universityId=1056&fieldId=1&periodId=5&fractionalCounting=1&performanceDimension=0&sizeIndependent=True&rankingIndicator=pp_top10&minNPubs=100</ref>  ANU is ranked first in the 4 Palmes category in the [[Eduniversal]] ranking.<ref>http://www.eduniversal-ranking.com/search/index</ref>

ANU counts six [[List of Nobel laureates|Nobel laureates]] among its [[List of Australian National University people|faculty and alumni]].<ref name="nobel">{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/profile/history/nobel-prize-winners|title=Nobel Prize winers|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> The university has educated two [[Prime Minister of Australia|prime ministers]], 30 current Australian [[Ambassador]]s and more than a dozen current heads of [[List of Australian government entities|Government departments of Australia]]. Students entering ANU in 2013 had a median [[Australian Tertiary Admission Rank]] of 93,<ref name="ATAR 1">{{cite web|url=http://docs.education.gov.au/system/files/doc/other/anu_2014-16_compact_final.docx|title=2014-16  Mission-based Compact: Commonwealth and ANU|publisher=Australian Government|accessdate=4 February 2014}}</ref><ref name="ATAR 2">{{cite web|url=http://vcdesk.anu.edu.au/2013/04/24/discussion-forum-videos/|title=University performance: where we are now (video)|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=4 February 2014}}</ref> the equal-highest among Australian universities.<ref name="ATAR 3">{{cite web|url=http://news.anu.edu.au/2013/10/25/a-strong-national-university-benefits-the-nation/|title=A strong national university benefits the nation|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=4 February 2014}}</ref>

==History==

==={{anchor|Australian Forestry School}}Post-war origins===
Calls for the establishment of a [[national university]] in Australia began as early as 1900.<ref name="history">{{cite web|url=http://heritage.anu.edu.au/?pid=7|title=History of ANU|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> After the location of the nation's capital, [[Canberra]], was determined in 1908, land was set aside for the university at the foot [[Black Mountain (Australian Capital Territory)|Black Mountain]] in the city designs by [[Walter Burley Griffin]].<ref name="history"/> Planning for the university was disrupted by [[World War II]] but resumed with the creation of the Department of Post-War Reconstruction in 1942, ultimately leading to the passage of the ''Australian National University Act 1946'' by the [[Parliament of Australia]] on 1 August 1946.<ref name="history"/>

[[File:Homopolar anu-MJC.jpg|thumb|220px|right|Remains of the ANU [[homopolar generator]] designed by [[Mark Oliphant#Later years in Australia|Mark Oliphant]]]]
A group of eminent Australian scholars returned from overseas to join the university, including Sir [[Howard Florey, Baron Florey|Howard Florey]] (co-developer of medicinal [[penicillin]]),  Sir [[Mark Oliphant]] (a nuclear physicist who worked on the [[Manhattan Project]]), Sir [[Keith Hancock]] (the [[Chichele Professor of Economic History]] at [[University of Oxford|Oxford]]) and Sir [[Raymond Firth]] (a professor of anthropology at [[London School of Economics|LSE]]).<ref name="history"/> Economist Sir [[Douglas Copland]] was appointed as ANU's first Vice-Chancellor and former Prime Minister [[Stanley Bruce]] served as the first Chancellor.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/profile/history/chancellors-vcs|title=Chancellors & Vice-Chancellors|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> ANU was originally organised into four centres—the Research Schools of Physical Sciences, Social Sciences and Pacific Studies and the [[John Curtin School of Medical Research]].<ref name="history"/>

The first residents’ hall, [[University House, Australian National University|University House]], was opened in 1954 for faculty members and postgraduate students.<ref name="timeline"/> [[Mount Stromlo Observatory]], established by the federal government in 1924, became part of ANU in 1957.<ref name="timeline"/> The first locations of the [[Australian National University Library|ANU Library]], the Menzies and Chifley buildings, opened in 1963.<ref name="timeline"/> The Australian Forestry School, located in Canberra since 1927, was amalgamated by ANU in 1965.<ref name="timeline"/>

==={{anchor|Canberra University College}} Canberra University College===
[[Canberra University College]] (CUC) was the first institution of higher education in the national capital, having been established in 1929 and enrolling its first undergraduate pupils in 1930.<ref name="history"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.legislation.act.gov.au/ord/1929-20/19291219-49059/pdf/1929-20.pdf|title=Ordinance establishing Canberra University College|work=ACT Legislation Register|publisher=[[Australian Capital Territory Legislative Assembly]]|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> Its founding was led by Sir [[Robert Garran]], one of the drafters of the [[Constitution of Australia|Australian Constitution]] and the first [[Solicitor-General of Australia]].<ref name="CUC">{{cite web|url=http://epress.anu.edu.au/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/ch1018.pdf|title=Canberra University College|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> CUC was affiliated with the [[University of Melbourne]] and its degrees were granted by that university.<ref name="history"/> Academic leaders at CUC included historian [[Manning Clark]], political scientist [[Finlay Crisp]], poet [[A. D. Hope]] and economist [[Heinz Arndt]].<ref name="CUC"/>

In 1960, CUC was integrated into ANU as the School of General Studies, initially with faculties in arts, economics, law and science.<ref name="timeline"/> Faculties in [[Oriental studies]] and engineering were introduced later.<ref name="timeline"/> Bruce Hall, the first residential college for undergraduates, opened in 1961.<ref name="timeline"/>
[[File:ANU School of Art.jpg|thumb|220px|right|ANU School of Art located at the former [[Canberra High School]] building]]

===Modern era===
The Canberra School of Music and the Canberra School of Art were amalgamated by ANU in 1992.<ref name="timeline"/>

ANU established its [[Australian National University Medical School|Medical School]] in 2002, after obtaining federal government approval in 2000.<ref name="medical">{{cite web|url=http://medicalschool.anu.edu.au/about-us/history|title=History of ANU Medical School|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

On 18 January 2003, the [[Canberra bushfires of 2003|Canberra bushfires]] largely destroyed the [[Mount Stromlo Observatory]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://rsaa.anu.edu.au/observatories/mount-stromlo-observatory|title=Mount Stromlo Observatory|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> ANU astronomers now conduct research from the [[Siding Spring Observatory]], which contains 10 telescopes including the [[Anglo-Australian Telescope]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://rsaa.anu.edu.au/observatories/siding-spring-observatory/telescopes|title=Telescopes|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

In February 2013, financial entrepreneur and ANU graduate Graham Tuckwell made the largest university donation in Australian history by giving $50 million to fund an undergraduate scholarship program at ANU.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.canberratimes.com.au/national/tertiary-education/billionaire-gives-back-with-50m-uni-donation-20130205-2dvfk.html|title=Billionaire gives back with $50m uni donation|publisher=The Canberra Times|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

ANU is well known for its history of [[student activism]] and, in recent years, its [[Fossil fuel divestment|fossil fuel divestment campaign]], which is one of the longest-running and most successful in the country.<ref>{{Cite news|title = Students put the Coalition on notice over climate change|url = http://www.theage.com.au/comment/students-put-the-coalition-on-notice-over-climate-change-20141022-119sn7.html|newspaper = The Age|date = 2014-10-23|access-date = 2016-01-26|first = Louis|last = Klee}}</ref> The decision of the ANU Council to divest from two fossil fuel companies in 2014 was criticised by ministers in the [[Abbott government]], but defended by Vice Chancellor Ian Young, who noted:<blockquote>On divestment, it is clear we were in the right and played a truly national and international leadership role. [...] [W]e seem to have played a major role in a movement which now seems unstoppable.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Farewell ANU|url = http://vcdesk.anu.edu.au/2015/12/17/farewell-anu/|date=17 December 2015|author=ANU Vice-Chancellor
|access-date = 2016-01-26}}</ref> </blockquote> As of 2014 ANU still had investments in major fossil fuel companies.<ref>{{Cite web|title = ANU sale of fossil fuel holdings not enough: students|url = http://www.abc.net.au/news/2014-10-03/anu-selling-fossil-fuel-company-holdings-not-enough-student-says/5789748|website = ABC News|access-date = 2016-01-26|language = en-AU}}</ref>

==Academics==
{{Main| Academic structure of the Australian National University}}
ANU is governed by a 15-member Council, whose members include the [[Chancellor (education)|Chancellor]] and [[Vice-Chancellor]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/governance-structure/council|title=ANU Council|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> [[Gareth Evans (politician)|Gareth Evans]], a former [[Minister for Foreign Affairs (Australia)|Foreign Minister of Australia]], has been ANU Chancellor since 2010 and Brian Schmidt, an astrophysicist and Nobel Laureate, became Vice-Chancellor on 1 January 2016.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/governance-structure/council/council-membership|title=Council membership|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> [[Ian Chubb]], Vice-Chancellor from 2001 to 2011, is now the [[Office of the Chief Scientist (Australia)|Chief Scientist of Australia]].

Undergraduate students are represented by the [[Australian National University Students' Association]] (ANUSA) and postgraduates by the Postgraduate and Research Students' Association (PARSA). The [[Australian National University Union]] manages catering and retail outlets and function amenities on behalf of all students.

In its most recent disclosure at the end of 2012, ANU recorded an endowment of A$1.13 billion.<ref name="endowment"/>

===Colleges===
ANU was reorganised in 2006 to create seven Colleges, each of which conducts both teaching and research.<ref name="timeline"/>

====Arts and Social Sciences====
The ANU College of Arts and Social Sciences is divided into the Research School of Social Sciences (RSSS) and the Research School of Humanities and the Arts (RSHA). Within RSSS there are schools dedicated to [[history]], [[philosophy]], [[sociology]], [[political science]] and [[international relations]], [[Middle Eastern studies]] and [[Latin American studies]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://rsss.anu.edu.au/schools-centres|title=RSSS Schools|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> RSHA contains schools focusing on [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[classics]], [[art history]], [[English literature]], [[drama]], [[film studies]], [[gender studies]], [[linguistics]], [[Languages of Europe|European languages]] as well as an [[art school|art]] and [[ANU School of Music|music school]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://rsha.anu.edu.au/schools-centres|title=RSHA Schools|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> In 2013, ANU ranked 9th in the world in linguistics and 16th in the world for modern languages.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://slll.anu.edu.au/about-us|title=About us|publisher=|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref>

====Asia and the Pacific====
The ANU College of Asia and the Pacific is a specialist centre of [[Asian studies|Asian]] and [[Pacific studies]] and languages, with the largest number of experts in these fields of any university in the English-speaking world.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://asiapacific.anu.edu.au/about-us/our-story|title=Our story - CAP|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> The College is home to three academic schools: the [[Crawford School of Public Policy]], a research intensive public policy school; the School of Culture History and Language, the nation's centre dedicated to investigating and learning with and about the people, languages, and lands of Asia and the Pacific; and Coral Bell School of Asia Pacific Affairs, the world's foremost collection of expertise in the politics and international affairs of Asia and the Pacific.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://asiapacific.anu.edu.au/about-us/college-glance|title=College at a glance - CAP|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=21 January 2016}}</ref> It also houses the Australian Centre on China in the World (CIW), the Regulatory Institutions Network (RegNet) and the [[CSCAP]] Australia.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ips.cap.anu.edu.au/sdsc/aus-cscap|title=Australian member committee of the Council for Security Cooperation in the Asia Pacific (Aus-CSCAP)|publisher=|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref>

====Business and Economics====
The ANU College of Business and Economics comprises four Research Schools, which in turn conduct research and teaching in [[economics]], [[finance]], [[accounting]], [[Actuarial science|actuarial studies]], [[statistics]], [[marketing]] and [[management]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://cbe.anu.edu.au/college/schools-centres/ |title=Schools & centres - ANU College of Business and Economics |publisher=Australian National University |accessdate=6 April 2013 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20130329002102/http://cbe.anu.edu.au:80/college/schools-centres/ |archivedate=29 March 2013 }}</ref> The college is professionally accredited with the Institute of Chartered Accountants, CPA Australia, the Australian Computer Society, the Actuaries Institute Australia, the Institute of Public Accountants, the Association of International Accountants, Chartered Financial Analyst Institute<ref>{{cite web|url=http://cbe.anu.edu.au/cbe/current-students/undergraduate-graduate-coursework/enrolment/professional-organisations-accreditation/|title=Professional organisations & accreditation|publisher=|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref> and attains membership of [[World Wide Web Consortium]].<ref>http://www.w3.org/Consortium/Member/List</ref>

====Engineering and Computer Science====
The [[ANU College of Engineering and Computer Science]] is divided into two Research Schools, which study a range of [[engineering]] and [[computer science]] topics respectively. ANU is home to the [[National Computational Infrastructure National Facility (Australia)|National Computational Infrastructure National Facility]] and was a co-founder of [[NICTA]], the chief [[information and communications technology]] research centre in Australia. Research groups in ANU College of Engineering and Computer Science include Algorithms and Data, Applied Signal Processing, Artificial Intelligence, Centre for Sustainable Energy Systems, Computer Systems, Computer Vision and Robotics, Data-Intensive Computing, Information and Human Centred Computing, Logic & Computation, Materials and Manufacturing, Semiconductor and Solar Cells, Software Intensive Systems Engineering, Solar Thermal Group, Systems and Control.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://cecs.anu.edu.au/research/groups|title=Research Groups  - CECS - ANU|publisher=|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref>

====Law====
The [[ANU College of Law]] conducts legal research and teaching, with centres dedicated to [[commercial law]], [[international law]], [[public law]] and [[environmental law]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://law.anu.edu.au/anu-college-law/anu-college-law-research-centres |title=ANU College of Law research centres |publisher=Australian National University |accessdate=6 April 2013 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20130514192019/http://law.anu.edu.au/anu-college-law/anu-college-law-research-centres |archivedate=14 May 2013 }}</ref> In addition to numerous research programs, the College offers the professional [[Bachelor of Laws|LL.B.]] and [[Juris Doctor|J.D.]] degrees. It is the 7th oldest<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.anu.edu.au/mac/images/uploads/ANU_Corporate_Brochure_web.pdf|title=ANU Brochure|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> of Australia's 36 [[List of law schools in Australia|law schools]] and was ranked 3rd among Australian and 14th among world law schools by the 2012 [[QS World University Rankings|QS Rankings]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.topuniversities.com/university-rankings/university-subject-rankings/2012/law-and-legal-studies|title=QS Rankings - Law|publisher=Quacquarelli Symonds|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> Students are given the chance to spend three weeks in Geneva concerning the institutional practice of International Law.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://programsandcourses.anu.edu.au/course/LAWS6258|title=International Organisations (Geneva)|publisher=|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref>

====Medicine, Biology and Environment====
The ANU College of Medicine, Biology and Environment encompasses the [[John Curtin School of Medical Research]] (JCSMR), the [[Australian National University Medical School|ANU Medical School]], the Fenner School of [[Environmental studies|Environment & Society]] and Research Schools of [[Biology]], [[Psychology]] and [[Population health|Population Health]].<ref name="smh">{{cite web|url=http://cmbe-cpms.anu.edu.au/about-us/research-schools-centres|title=About us - Science, Medicine & Health|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> JCSMR was established in 1948 as a result of the vision of Nobel laureate [[Howard Florey]].<ref name="jcsmr">{{cite web|url=http://jcsmr.anu.edu.au/about-us|title=About us - JCSMR|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> Three further Nobel Prizes have been won as a result of research at JCSMR—in 1963 by [[John Eccles (neurophysiologist)|John Eccles]] and in 1996 by [[Peter C. Doherty|Peter Doherty]] and [[Rolf M. Zinkernagel]].

====Physical and Mathematical Sciences====
The [[ANU College of Physical & Mathematical Sciences]] comprises the Research Schools of [[Astronomy]] & [[Astrophysics]], [[Chemistry]], [[Earth science|Earth Sciences]], [[Mathematics|Mathematical Sciences]] and [[Physics]]. Under the direction of [[Mark Oliphant]], nuclear physics was one the university's most notable early research priorities, leading to the construction of a 500 megajoule [[homopolar generator]] and a 7.7 megaelectronvolts [[cyclotron]] in the 1950s.<ref name="physics">{{cite web|url=http://physics.anu.edu.au/fire_in_the_belly/Fire_in_the_Belly03.pdf|title=The Big Machine|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> These devices were to be used as part of a 10.6 gigaelectronvolt [[synchrotron]] [[particle accelerator]] that was never completed, however they remained in use for other research purposes.<ref name="physics"/> ANU has been home to eight particle accelerators over the years and operates the 14UD and LINAS accelerators.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://physics.anu.edu.au/fire_in_the_belly/Fire_in_the_Belly14.pdf|title=The Accelerators of Nuclear Physics|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> Professor [[Brian Schmidt]] (astrophysicist at [[Mount Stromlo Observatory]]) received the 2011 [[Nobel Prize for Physics]] for his work on the accelerating expansion of the universe.

==Campus==
The main campus of ANU extends across the [[Canberra]] suburb of [[Acton, Australian Capital Territory|Acton]], which consists of {{convert|358|acre|km2}} of mostly parkland with university buildings landscaped within.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.anu.edu.au/about/tour/|title=ANU Tour|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> ANU is roughly bisected by [[Sullivans Creek]], part of the [[Murray–Darling basin]], and is bordered by the native bushland of [[Black Mountain (Australian Capital Territory)|Black Mountain]], [[Lake Burley Griffin]], the suburb of [[Turner, Australian Capital Territory|Turner]] and the Canberra [[City Centre, Australian Capital Territory|central business district]]. Many university sites are of historical significance dating from the establishment of the national capital, with over 40 buildings recognised by the Commonwealth Heritage List and several others on local lists.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://heritage.anu.edu.au/files/1602_ANU%20Heritage%20Strategy%202010-12.pdf |title=ANU Heritage Strategy |publisher=Australian National University |accessdate=6 April 2013 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20130411131743/http://heritage.anu.edu.au/files/1602_ANU%20Heritage%20Strategy%202010-12.pdf |archivedate=11 April 2013 }}</ref>

With over 10,000 trees on its campus,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://heritage.anu.edu.au/index.php?pid=128 |title=Acton Tree Trail |publisher=Australian National University |accessdate=6 April 2013 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20120319002146/http://heritage.anu.edu.au/index.php?pid=128 |archivedate=19 March 2012 }}</ref> ANU won an International Sustainable Campus Network Award in 2009<ref>{{cite web|url=http://facilities.anu.edu.au/about-us/achievements-and-awards/sustainability-awards|title=Sustainability awards|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> and was ranked the 2nd greenest university campus in Australia in 2011.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.theaustralian.com.au/higher-education/australias-10-greenest-universities/story-e6frgcjx-1226215407489|title=Australia's 10 greenest universities|publisher=The Australian|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

Four of Australia's five [[Learned society|learned societies]] are based at ANU—the [[Australian Academy of Science]], the [[Australian Academy of the Humanities]], the [[Academy of the Social Sciences in Australia]] and the Australian Academy of Law. The [[Australian National Centre for the Public Awareness of Science]] and the [[National Film and Sound Archive]] are also located at ANU, while the [[National Museum of Australia]] and [[Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation|CSIRO]] are situated next to the campus.

ANU occupies additional locations including [[Mount Stromlo Observatory]] on the outskirts of Canberra, [[Siding Spring Observatory]] near [[Coonabarabran, New South Wales|Coonabarabran]], a campus at [[Kioloa Coastal Campus|Kioloa]] on the [[South Coast, New South Wales|South Coast]] of New South Wales and a research unit in [[Darwin, Northern Territory|Darwin]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://about.anu.edu.au/campus|title=Campus - About ANU|publisher=Australian National University|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

===Library===
{{Main| Australian National University Library}}
The library of ANU originated in 1948 with the appointment of the first librarian, Arthur McDonald.<ref name="timeline"/> The library holds over 2.5 million physical volumes<ref>{{cite web|url=http://anulib.anu.edu.au/using-the-library/collections/|title=Collections - ANU Library|publisher=The Australian|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref> distributed across six branches—the Chifley, Menzies, Hancock, Art & Music, and Law Libraries and the external Print Repository.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://anulib.anu.edu.au/using-the-library/branches/|title=Branches- ANU Library|publisher=The Australian|accessdate=6 April 2013}}</ref>

===Residential halls and colleges===
Eight residential facilities are affiliated with ANU—Bruce Hall, Ursula Hall, Burgmann College, John XXIII College, Toad Hall, Burton & Garran Hall, Graduate House and Fenner Hall.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.anu.edu.au/res/ |title=Residential Halls and Colleges |publisher=Australian National University |accessdate=6 April 2013 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20120426210134/http://www.anu.edu.au/res/ |archivedate=26 April 2012 }}</ref> All are located on campus except Fenner Hall, which is located in the nearby suburb of [[Braddon, Australian Capital Territory|Braddon]]. Students also reside in the privately run units adjoining the campus—Davey Lodge, Kinloch Lodge, Warrumbul Lodge and Lena Karmel Lodge. In 2010, the non-residential Griffin Hall was established for students living off-campus. Another off-campus student accommodation was launched by UniGardens Pty, University Gardens<ref name="UniGardens Canberra student accommodation">[http://unigardens.com.au/ UniGardens Canberra ANU student accommodation], official website</ref> located in [[Belconnen, Australian Capital Territory|Belconnen]].

Notable past faculty include [[Mark Oliphant]], [[Keith Hancock]], [[Manning Clark]], [[Derek Freeman]], [[H. C. Coombs]], [[Hedley Bull]] and [[Frank Fenner]]. [[Nobel Prize]]s have been awarded to former ANU Chancellor [[Howard Florey]] and faculty members [[John Eccles (neurophysiologist)|John Eccles]], [[John Harsanyi]], [[Rolf M. ANU alumni are especially visible in government. [[Bob Hawke]] and [[Kevin Rudd]], former Australian [[Prime Minister of Australia|Prime Ministers]], attended the university, as did senior politicians [[Barry O'Farrell]], [[Nick Minchin]], [[Kim Edward Beazley|Kim Beazley Sr]], [[Peter Garrett]], [[Craig Emerson]], [[Stephen Conroy]], [[Gary Gray (Australian politician)|Gary Gray]], [[Warren Snowdon]], [[Joe Ludwig]] and [[Catherine King (politician)|Catherine King]] and [[Michael Keenan]]. ANU has produced 30 current Australian [[Ambassador]]s and more than a dozen current heads of [[Australian Public Service]] departments, including [[Department of the Prime Minister and Cabinet (Australia)|Prime Minister & Cabinet]] secretary [[Michael Thawley]], [[Department of the Treasury (Australia)|Treasury]] secretary [[Martin Parkinson]], [[Department of Finance (Australia)|Finance]] secretary [[Jane Halton]], [[Department of Education (Australia)|Education]] secretary [[Lisa Paul]], [[Department of Agriculture (Australia)|Agriculture]] secretary [[Paul Grimes (public servant)|Paul Grimes]], [[Attorney-General's Department (Australia)|Attorney-General's]] secretary Chris Moraitis, [[Department of the Environment (Australia)|Environment]] secretary Gordon de Brouwer, [[Department of Employment (Australia)|Employment]] secretary Renee Leon, [[Department of Social Services (Australia)|Social Services]] secretary Finn Pratt, [[Department of Industry (Australia)|Industry]] secretary Glenys Beauchamp, [[Australian Secret Intelligence Service|ASIS]] director-general [[Nick Warner]] and [[Australian Competition and Consumer Commission|ACCC]] chairman [[Rod Sims]]. Graduates also include [[Prime Minister of the Solomon Islands]] [[Gordon Darcy Lilo]], former Indonesian Foreign Minister [[Marty Natalegawa]], former Governor of the [[Reserve Bank of New Zealand]] [[Don Brash]], former British cabinet minister [[Patricia Hewitt]] and former U.S. Ambassador to Israel [[Martin Indyk]].

Other notable alumni include [[High Court of Australia]] judges [[Stephen Gageler]] and [[Geoffrey Nettle]], [[Chief Federal Magistrate of Australia|Chief Federal Magistrate]] [[John Pascoe]], human rights lawyer [[Jennifer Robinson (lawyer)|Jennifer Robinson]], [[Kellogg Company|Kellogg's]] CEO [[John A. Bryant (businessman)|John Bryant]], former [[Singapore Airlines]] CEO [[Cheong Choong Kong]], [[Indiana University]] president [[Michael McRobbie]], [[University of Melbourne]] Vice-Chancellors [[Alan Gilbert (Australian academic)|Alan Gilbert]] and [[Glyn Davis]], mathematician [[John H. Coates]], public intellectual [[Clive Hamilton]] and economists [[Ross Garnaut]], [[Peter Drysdale]] and [[John Quiggin]].

==Affiliations==
ANU is a member of the [[Group of Eight (Australian universities)|Group of Eight]] and the [[International Alliance of Research Universities]]. As Australia’s only member of this association, ANU enjoys close relationships and exchange partnerships with the [[University of Cambridge]], [[University of Oxford]], [[University of California, Berkeley]], [[Yale University]], [[Peking University]], [[National University of Singapore]], [[University of Tokyo]], [[University of Copenhagen]] and [[ETH Zurich]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.iaruni.org/about-us/members|title=Members - IARU|publisher=|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.anu.edu.au/study/student-experience/international-exchange|title=International exchange|work=ANU|accessdate=2 June 2015}}</ref>
