Counties of Romania

{{Romanian counties clickable|width=600px}}
A total of '''41 counties''' ({{lang-ro|[[județ]]e}}), along with the [[municipality]] of [[Bucharest]], constitute the official [[administrative divisions of Romania]]. They represent [[Romania|the country]]'s NUTS-3 ([[Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics]] – Level 3) statistical subdivisions within the [[European Union]] and each of them serves as the local level of [[Government of Romania|government]] within its borders. Most counties are named after a major [[Rivers in Romania|river]], while some are named after notable cities within them, such as the county seat.

The earliest organization into ''județe'' of the Principalities of [[Wallachia]] and [[Moldavia]] (where they were termed ''ținuturi'') dates back to at least the late 14th century. For most of the time since [[National awakening of Romania|modern Romania was formed]] in 1859, the administrative division system has been similar to the [[Departments of France|French departments]] one. The system has been changed several times since then, and the number of counties has varied over time, from the 71 ''județe'' that existed before [[World War II]] to only 39 after 1968. The current format has largely been in place since 1968 as only small changes have been made since then, the last of which was in 1997.

According to 2011 census data from the [[National Institute of Statistics (Romania)|National Institute of Statistics]], the average population of Romania's 41 counties is about 445,000, with [[Iași County]] as the most populous (772,000) and [[Covasna County]] (210,000) the least. The average county's land area is {{convert|5809|km2}}, with [[Timiș County]] ({{convert|8697|km2}}) the largest and [[Ilfov County]] ({{convert|1583|km2}}) the smallest. The municipality of Bucharest, which has the same administrative level as that of a county, is both more populous and much smaller than any county, with 1,883,425 people and {{convert|228|km2}}.

==History==
[[File:Romania 1930 counties.500px.svg|thumb|350px|The 71 counties of Romania between 1925 and 1940|alt=Colored map showing the territory of Romania and its division into 71 counties before the World War II.]]
[[Image:Romania Counties 1930-2008.svg|thumb|350px|Current counties imposed over the inter-war counties|alt=Outline showing the territory of present Romania and its into counties superimposed over the colored map of the inter-war counties.]]
{{main|Former administrative divisions of Romania}}
The [[Lex Antiqua Valachorum|earliest organization]] into ''[[județ]]e'' (for [[Wallachia]]), and ''ținuturi'' (for [[Moldavia]]), dates back at least to the late 14th century.<ref group=note>''[[Județ]]'' originates from the Latin ''judicium'' and ''ținut'' probably from the Latin ''tenutum''.</ref><ref name=Vl>{{cite web|url=http://www.valcea.insse.ro/main.php?lang=fr&pageid=509 |title=Primele atestari documentare ale judetului Valcea (First Historical Mentions of Vâlcea County) | publisher=[[National Institute of Statistics (Romania)]]| language=Romanian|date= |accessdate=2010-01-23}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|url=http://www.verticalonline.ro/de-la-sat-la-judet-in-istoria-olteniei |title=De la sat la județ în istoria Olteniei (From Village to County in the History of Oltenia) |author=Cornel Șomâcu|publisher=''Vertical''|language=Romanian|issn=1841-6063 |date=2009-09-09 |accessdate=2010-08-22}}</ref><ref name= Dariescu>{{cite book |author=Cosmin Dariescu|title=Istoria statului și dreptului românesc din antichitate până la Marea Unire (History of Romanian State and Law from Antiquity until the Unification)|publisher=C.H. Beck|year=2008|pages=47–51|language=Romanian|chapter=21: Organizarea administrativ-teritorială a Țării Românești și a Moldovei în evul mediu (21: Administrative Divisions in Wallachia and Moldavia in the Middle Ages)|isbn=978-973-115-337-7}}</ref>  Inspired from the organization of the late [[Byzantine Empire]], each ''județ'' was ruled by a ''jude'' (or ''pârcălab'' for a ''ținut''), a person officially appointed with administrative and judicial functions.<ref name= Dariescu/><ref>{{cite book |author=Cosmin Dariescu|title=Istoria statului și dreptului românesc din antichitate până la Marea Unire (History of Romanian State and Law from Antiquity until the Unification)|publisher=C.H. Beck|year=2008|pages=35–39|language=Romanian|chapter=16: Domnia în Țările Române medievale (16: Rulers in the Romanian Principalities in the Middle Ages) |isbn=978-973-115-337-7}}</ref> [[Transylvania]] was divided into [[Comitatus (Kingdom of Hungary)|royal counties]] headed by ''comes'' (royal counts) with administrative and judicial functions.<ref name= Dariescu/>

After modern Romania was formed in 1859 through the [[United Principalities|union of Wallachia and the rump of Moldavia]], the administrative division was modernized using the French administrative system as a model, with ''județ'' as the basic administrative unit.<ref name=Ag/><ref name=UNDP/> Aside from the 1950–1968 period, this system has remained in place until today. Since 1864, for each ''județ'' there exists a ''[[Prefect (Romania)|prefect]]'', a subordinate of the [[Ministry of Administration and Interior (Romania)|Ministry of Internal Affairs]] and representative of the government inside the county; he is also the head of local administration for areas not delegated to local authorities.<ref name=Ag>{{cite web|url=http://www.prefecturaarges.ro:8080/prefectura/index.jsp?pagina=scurt_istoric|language=Romanian|title=Scurtă privire istorică (Short View on History)|publisher= Instituția Prefectului județul Argeș |date= |accessdate=2010-01-22}}</ref><ref name=UNDP/> Until 1948, each ''județ'' was further divided into several ''[[Plasă|plăși]]'', each administered by a ''pretor''.<ref name=Bz>{{cite web|author=Valeriu Nicolescu |url=http://www.buzau.com/judet.htm |title=Un ținut de legendă, județul Buzău (A legendary Land, Buzau County) |publisher=Buzau.com |language=Romanian|date= |accessdate=2010-01-22}}</ref>

After the adoption of a [[1923 Constitution of Romania|new Constitution]] in 1923, the traditional local administrative systems of the newly acquired regions of [[Transylvania]], [[Bukovina]] and [[Bessarabia]] were made uniform in 1925 with that of the [[Romanian Old Kingdom]]. County borders were kept largely intact, with few adjustments, and the total number of counties was raised to 71; this lasted until the beginning of [[World War II]].<ref name=UNDP>{{cite report|url=http://hdr.undp.org/en/reports/nationalreports/europethecis/romania/romania_2005_en.pdf|title=2003–2005 National Human Development Report: Local Governance in Romania|publisher=United Nations Development Programme|page=35|format=PDF}}</ref>

In 1938, [[Carol II of Romania|King Carol II]] modified the law on the administration of the Romanian territory according to the [[Fascism|fascist]] model.<ref>{{cite encyclopedia|author=Günther H. Tontsch|language=German|issn=0937-7107|encyclopedia=Jahrbuch für Europäische Verwaltungsgeschichte (Yearbook of European Administrative History)|title=Juristische Literatur zur rumänischen Verwaltungsgeschichte im 19. und 20. Jahrhundert (Law Literature on the Romanian Administrative History in the 19th and 20th Centuries) |volume=12|year=2000|pages=285|publisher=Nomos Verlag|location=Baden-Baden}}</ref> Ten ''ținuturi'' (approx. translation "lands") were created, ruled by ''Rezidenți Regali'' (Royal Residents), appointed directly by the [[King of Romania|Monarch]]. The ''ținuturi'' represented another layer of administration between counties and the country, as the county borders were not erased.<ref name=Ag/><ref>{{cite book|language=Romanian|author1=Ioan Scurtu |author2=Theodora Stănescu-Stanciu |author3=Georgiana Margareta Scurtu |title=Istoria românilor între anii 1918–1940 (The History of the Romanians in 1918–1940)|publisher=University of Bucharest|year=2002|url=http://ebooks.unibuc.ro/istorie/istorie1918-1940/8-7.htm|chapter=8.7. Decret-lege pentru reforma electorală (Law Decree for electoral reform)}}</ref>

Due to the territorial changes during World War II, this style of administration did not last, and the administration at the ''județ'' level was reintroduced after the war.<ref name=Ag/> Between 1941–1944, Romania administered the territory between the [[Dniester]] and [[Southern Bug]] rivers known as [[Transnistria (World War II)|Transnistria]], which consisted of 13 separate counties.<ref>{{cite book|isbn=978-9975-69-049-2|language=Romanian|author=Anatol Petrenci|title=Basarabia în timpul celui de-al doilea război mondial: 1939–1945 (Bessarabia During the Second World War: 1939–1945) |publisher=Ed. Prut Internațional|year=2006|location=Chișinău, Moldova}}</ref>

After taking over the administration of the country in 1945, the [[Romanian Communist Party|Communist Party]] changed the administrative model to [[Subdivisions of the Soviet Union|that of the Soviet Union]] (regions and [[raion]]s) in 1950, but changed it back in 1968.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.vestul.ro/stiri/1310/in-linii-mari.htm |title=Istoria Banatului – În linii mari (History of Banat – A Quick View)|publisher=Vestul.ro |date=2008-09-10 |accessdate=2010-10-22|language=Romanian}}</ref> Nevertheless, the county borders set then were quite different from those present during the [[interbellum]], as only 39 counties were formed from the 56 remaining after the war.<ref name=rolib>{{cite web|url= http://www.romanialibera.ro/opinii/interviuri/cum-ar-trebui-sa-arate-harta-redesenata-a-romaniei-179771.html  |title=Cum ar trebui să arate harta redesenată a României? (How Should Romania's Redrawn Map Look Like?) |publisher= ''[[România Liberă]]'' |date=2010-03-11 |author=Petre Mihai Bacanu |language=Romanian | accessdate=2010-08-29}}</ref>

In 1981, [[Giurgiu County|Giurgiu]] and [[Călărași County|Călărași]] were split from [[Ialomița County|Ialomița]] and the former county of Ilfov,<ref name=rolib/> while in 1997, [[Ilfov County]], which had been a dependency of the municipality of Bucharest for nearly two decades, was reinstated.<ref>{{cite web|url= http://www.ilfov.insse.ro/phpfiles/PREZENTAREA%20JUDETULUI%20ILFOV.pdf|title=Prezentarea Judetului Ilfov (Overview of Ilfov County) |publisher=National Institute of Statistics (Romania)|language=Romanian |format=PDF |date= |accessdate=2010-08-01}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ccilfov.ro/istoria_ilfov.php |publisher=Camera de Comert si Industrie a Judetului Ilfov |title=Istoria Ilfov (History of Ilfov) |language=Romanian|date= |accessdate=2010-08-23}}</ref> The county borders set in 1968 are still largely in place today, but the functions of different authorities have changed due to administrative reforms in the 1990s.<ref name=Ag/><ref name=UNDP/>

At present, [[Romania]] is divided into 41 counties and one municipality (Bucharest); these are assigned as the [[NUTS of Romania|NUTS-3]] [[geocode]] statistical subdivision scheme of Romania within the [[European Union]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu/portal/page/portal/nuts_nomenclature/introduction |title=Nomenclature of territorial units for statistics – Introduction |publisher=[[Eurostat]] |date= |accessdate=2010-08-22}}</ref> Each of the counties is further divided into [[List of cities in Romania|cities]] (some of which have [[Municipalities of Romania|municipality]] status) and [[Communes of Romania|communes]]. The ''prefect'' and his administration have executive prerogatives within the county limits, while limited legislative powers are assigned to a County Council elected every four years during local elections.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ccre.org/roumanie_en.htm |title=CEMR – Members – Romania |publisher=Council of European Municipalities and Regions |date= |accessdate=2010-08-22}}</ref> The territorial districts of the [[Courts in Romania|Romanian judicial system]] overlap with county borders, thus avoiding further complication in the [[separation of powers]] on the government.<ref name=Ag/>
