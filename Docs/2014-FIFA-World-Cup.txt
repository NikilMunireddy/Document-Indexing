The '''2014 FIFA World Cup''' was the 20th [[FIFA World Cup]], the quadrennial world championship for [[List of men's national association football teams|men's national]] [[association football|football]] teams organized by [[FIFA]]. It took place in Brazil from 12 June to 13 July 2014, after the country was awarded the hosting rights in 2007. It was the second time that Brazil staged the competition (the first was in [[1950 FIFA World Cup|1950]]), and the fifth time that it was held in South America.

Thirty-one national teams advanced through [[2014 FIFA World Cup qualification|qualification competitions]] to join the host nation in the final tournament. A total of 64 matches were played in 12 venues located in as many host cities across Brazil. For the first time at a World Cup finals, match officials used [[goal-line technology]], as well as [[vanishing foam]] for [[Direct free kick|free kicks]].<ref name="GLT">{{cite web |title=FIFA launch GLT tender for Brazil 2013/14 |url=http://www.fifa.com/aboutfifa/organisation/marketing/qualityprogramme/news/newsid=2014983/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=19 February 2013 }}</ref> [[FIFA Fan Fest]]s in each host city gathered a total of 5 million people, and the country received 1 million visitors from 202 countries.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://g1.globo.com/politica/noticia/2014/07/em-balanco-da-copa-dilma-afirma-que-brasil-derrotou-pessimistas.html|title=G1 - Em balanço da Copa, Dilma diz que Brasil derrotou prognósticos 'terríveis' - notícias em Política|language=pt|work=Política}}</ref>

Every World Cup-winning team since the [[1930 FIFA World Cup|first edition in 1930]] – [[Argentina national football team|Argentina]], [[Brazil national football team|Brazil]], [[England national football team|England]], [[France national football team|France]], [[Germany national football team|Germany]], [[Italy national football team|Italy]], [[Spain national football team|Spain]] and [[Uruguay national football team|Uruguay]] – qualified for this tournament. Spain, the title holders, were eliminated at the group stage, along with England and Italy. Uruguay were eliminated in the round of 16, and France exited in the quarter-finals. Brazil, who had won the [[2013 FIFA Confederations Cup]], [[Brazil v Germany (2014 FIFA World Cup)|lost to Germany]] in the semi-finals and eventually finished in fourth place. In [[2014 FIFA World Cup Final|the final]], Germany defeated Argentina 1–0 to win the tournament and secure the country's fourth world title, the first after the [[German reunification]] in 1990, when as West Germany they also beat Argentina in the [[1990 FIFA World Cup Final|World Cup final]].
Germany became the first European team to win a World Cup staged in the [[Americas]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://espnfc.com/blog/_/name/worldcupcentral/id/53?cc=5739|title=If the World Cup started tomorrow|publisher=ESPN |date=12 June 2013}}</ref> and this result marked the first time that sides from the same continent had won three consecutive tournaments (following Italy in [[2006 FIFA World Cup|2006]] and Spain in [[2010 FIFA World Cup|2010]]).<ref>{{cite web|last1=Owen|first1=David|title=Battle of the Brands: Adidas lifts the World Cup, but Nike scores most goals|url=http://www.insideworldfootball.com/world-cup/brazil-2014/15059-battle-of-the-brands-adidas-lifts-the-world-cup-but-nike-scores-the-most-goals|website=Inside World Football|accessdate=14 July 2014}}</ref><ref name=Trivia>{{cite web|title=Numbers Game: All the stats from Germany's fourth World Cup triumph|url=http://www.firstpost.com/sports/numbers-game-stats-germanys-fourth-world-cup-triumph-1617441.html|website=Firstpost|accessdate=14 July 2014}}</ref>

As the winners, Germany qualified for the [[2017 FIFA Confederations Cup]] in Russia.

==Host selection==
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup bids}}
[[File:Joseph Blatter - World Cup 2014.jpg|thumb|Announcing of Brazil as hosts, 2007]]
In March 2003, FIFA announced that the tournament would be held in South America for the first time since [[1978 FIFA World Cup|1978]], in line with its then-active policy of rotating the right to host the World Cup among different confederations.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/newscentre/news/newsid=85909/index.html|title=2014 FIFA World Cup to be held in South America|publisher=FIFA |date=7 March 2003}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Rotation ends in 2018 |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/russia2018/organisation/media/newsid=625122/ |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=29 October 2007 }}</ref> With [[2010 FIFA World Cup]] hosted in South Africa, it would be the second consecutive World Cup outside of Europe, which was a first for the tournament. It was also second in the Southern Hemisphere.<ref>{{cite web|title=Brazil may lose the right to hold World Cup 2014|url=http://english.pravda.ru/news/sports/15-05-2013/124564-brazil_world_cup_2014-0/|website=Pravda.Ru|accessdate=25 June 2014}}</ref> Only Brazil and Colombia formally declared their candidacy but, after the withdrawal of the latter from the process,<ref>{{cite web |title=Brazil confirms bid – Colombia withdraws |url=http://www.fifa.com/world-match-centre/news/newsid/123/706/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=13 April 2007}}</ref> Brazil was officially elected as host nation unopposed on 30 October 2007.<ref>{{cite web |title=Brazil confirmed as 2014 hosts |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2007/m=10/news=brazil-confirmed-2014-hosts-625695.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=30 October 2007 }}</ref>

===Qualification===
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup qualification}}
{{See also|FIFA World Cup qualification}}
Following qualification matches played between June 2011 and November 2013, the following 32 teams – shown with their last pre-tournament [[FIFA World Rankings|FIFA world ranking]]<ref name="Rankings">{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldranking/index.html |title=FIFA/Coca-Cola Rankings |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=5 June 2014 |accessdate=11 June 2014 }}</ref> – qualified for the final tournament. Twenty-four of these teams were returning participants from the [[2010 FIFA World Cup|2010 World Cup]]. [[Bosnia and Herzegovina national football team|Bosnia and Herzegovina]] were the only team with no previous appearance at the World Cup finals.{{refn|group=nb|Bosnia and Herzegovina was until 1992 part of Yugoslavia, which competed at eight World Cup tournaments.}}<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=6/news=1-day-to-go-2363325.html|title=1 day to go|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association)|date=11 June 2014 }}</ref> [[Colombia national football team|Colombia]] qualified for the World Cup after [[1998 FIFA World Cup|16 years of absence]], while [[Russia national football team|Russia]] and [[Belgium national football team|Belgium]] returned after 12 years. The highest ranked team to not qualify was [[Ukraine national football team|Ukraine]] (ranked 16th), while the lowest ranked team that did qualify was [[Australia national football team|Australia]] (ranked 62nd).<ref name="Rankings"/>

===Final draw===
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup seeding}}
The 32 participating teams were drawn into eight groups. In preparation for this, the teams were organised into four pots with the seven [[FIFA World Rankings|highest-ranked]] teams joining host nation [[Brazil national football team|Brazil]] in the seeded pot.<ref>{{cite news |title=Pot 1 seeds set for Brazil 2014 draws |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldranking/news/newsid=2197884/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=17 October 2013 |accessdate=23 November 2013 }}</ref> As with the previous tournaments, FIFA aimed to create groups which maximised geographic separation and therefore the unseeded teams were arranged into pots based on geographic considerations.<ref name="Draw procedures">{{cite news |title=Draw procedures approved |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/final-draw/news/newsid=2238498/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=3 December 2013 |accessdate=3 December 2013 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Final draw procedures |url=http://www.fifa.com/mm/document/tournament/finaldraw/02/23/84/73/131203_finaldrawprocedures_neutral.pdf |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=3 December 2013 |accessdate=3 December 2013 }}</ref> The draw took place on 6 December 2013 at the Costa do Sauípe resort in [[Bahia]], during which the teams were drawn by various past World Cup-winning players.<ref>{{cite news |title=Final Draw reveals intriguing groups |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2013/m=12/news=final-draw-reveals-intriguing-groups-2240026.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=6 December 2013 }}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=A team of stars for the draw |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2013/m=12/news=team-stars-for-the-draw-2239896.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=5 December 2013 }}</ref> Under the draw procedure, one randomly drawn team – Italy – was firstly relocated from Pot 4 to Pot 2 to create four equal pots of eight teams.<ref name="Draw procedures"/>

===Officials===
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup officials}}
In March 2013, FIFA published a list of 52 prospective referees, each paired, on the basis of nationality, with two [[Assistant referee (association football)|assistant referee]]s, from all six football confederations for the tournament. On 14 January 2014, the FIFA Referees Committee appointed 25 referee trios and eight support duos representing 43 different countries for the tournament.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=1/news=referee-trios-and-eight-support-duos-representing-different-countries-ap-2261111.html|title=Referee trios and support duos appointed for 2014 FIFA World Cup|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=15 January 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://resources.fifa.com/mm/document/tournament/competition/02/26/13/55/2014fwcrefereesselected_neutral.pdf|title=Referees & assistant referees for the 2014 FIFA World Cup|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) }}</ref>
[[Yuichi Nishimura]] from [[Japan Football Association|Japan]] acted as referee in the opening match whereas [[Nicola Rizzoli]] from [[Italian Football Federation|Italy]] acted as referee in the final.<ref name="Matches 1-4">{{cite web|url=http://resources.fifa.com/mm/document/tournament/competition/02/36/68/33/140610_1-4_neutral.pdf|title=Referee designations for matches 1–4|date=10 June 2014|work=fifa.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/matches/round=255959/match=300186501/report.html|title=2014 FIFA World Cup™ - Matches - FIFA.com|work=FIFA.com}}</ref>

===Squads===
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup squads}}

As with the [[2010 FIFA World Cup squads|2010 tournament]], each team's squad consists of 23 players (three of whom must be goalkeepers). Each participating national association had to confirm their final 23-player squad no later than 10 days before the start of the tournament.<ref name="regulations">{{cite web |title=Regulations&nbsp;– FIFA World Cup Brazil 2014 |url=http://www.fifa.com/mm/document/tournament/competition/01/47/38/17/regulationsfwcbrazil2014_update_e_neutral.pdf |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association)}}</ref> Teams were permitted to make late replacements in the event of serious injury, at any time up to 24 hours before their first game.<ref name="regulations"/> During a match, all remaining squad members not named in the starting team are available to be one of the three permitted substitutions (provided the player is not serving a suspension).<ref name="regulations"/>

==Venues==
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup venues}}

12 venues (seven new and five renovated) in twelve cities were selected for the tournament. The venues covered all the main [[regions of Brazil]] and created more evenly distributed hosting than the [[1950 FIFA World Cup|1950 finals]] in Brazil.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://globoesporte.globo.com/platb/memoriaec/2009/05/31/sedes-da-copa-de-1950-voltam-no-mundial-de-2014/ |title=Host cities in 1950 FIFA World Cup |publisher=Colunas.globoesporte.com |accessdate=9 October 2011 }}</ref> Consequently, the tournament required long-distance travel for teams.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/sport/0/football/25187878 |title=2014 FIFA World Cup: Luck of the draw 'an essential component' |date=2 December 2013 |publisher=BBC Sport (British Broadcasting Corporation) }}</ref> During the World Cup, Brazilian cities were also home to the participating teams at [[2014 FIFA World Cup venues#Team base camps|32 separate base camps]],<ref name=autogenerated2>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=2267880/ |title=Team Base Camps for Brazil 2014 announced |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=21 January 2014 }}</ref> as well as staging [[FIFA Fan Fest#2014|official fan fests]] where supporters could view the games.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=1614471/ |title=FIFA Fan Fest locations confirmed |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=20 January 2014 }}</ref>

===Team base camps===
Base camps were used by the 32 national squads to stay and train before and during the World Cup tournament. On 31 January 2014, FIFA announced the base camps for each participating team,<ref name=autogenerated2 /> having earlier circulated a brochure of 84 prospective locations.<ref>{{cite web |title=Team Base Camps Brochure |url=http://resources.fifa.com/mm/document/tournament/loc/01/67/33/67/tbc_web_finalversion071113_neutral.pdf |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=7 November 2013 }}</ref> Most teams opted to stay in the [[Southeast Region, Brazil|Southeast Region]] of Brazil, with only eight teams choosing other regions; five teams (Croatia, Germany, Ghana, Greece and Switzerland) opted to stay in the [[Northeast Region, Brazil|Northeast Region]] and three teams (Ecuador, South Korea and Spain) opted to stay in the [[South Region, Brazil|South Region]]. None opted to stay in the [[North Region, Brazil|North Region]] or the [[Central-West Region, Brazil|Central-West Region]].<ref>{{cite web |title=Most teams pick bases in south east Brazil |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/sport/0/football/25998576 |publisher=BBC Sport (British Broadcasting Corporation) |date=1 February 2014 }}</ref>

===FIFA Fan Fests===
For a third consecutive World Cup tournament, FIFA staged [[FIFA Fan Fest]]s in each of the 12 host cities throughout the competition. Prominent examples were the [[Copacabana Beach]] in Rio de Janeiro, which already held a Fan Fest in 2010, and [[São Paulo]]'s Vale do Anhangabaú.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://worldcupfancamp.com/viewtopic.php?f=118&t=10273 |title=World Cup 2014 FanCamps and FanFests |accessdate=8 August 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=1614471/ |title=FIFA Fan Fest locations confirmed |accessdate=20 January 2014}}</ref> The first official event took place on [[Praia de Iracema|Iracema Beach]], in [[Fortaleza]], on 8 June 2014.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=6/news=ronaldo-kicks-off-fifa-fan-fest-2360141.html|title=Ronaldo kicks off FIFA Fan Fest|date=9 June 2014}}</ref>

==Innovations==
===Technologies===
To avoid [[ghost goal]]s the 2014 World Cup introduced [[goal-line technology]] following successful trials at among others [[2013 FIFA Confederations Cup|2013 Confederations Cup]]. The chosen [[GoalControl|Goal Control system]] featured 14 high speed cameras, 7 directed to each of the goals. Data were sent to the central image-processing centre, where a virtual representation of the ball was output on a widescreen to confirm the goal. The referee was equipped with a watch which vibrated and displayed a signal upon a goal.<ref name=DW>{{cite web|title=Behind the Scenes with GoalControl|url=http://www.dw.de/behind-the-scenes-with-goalcontrol/av-17711403|website=[[Deutsche Welle]]|accessdate=14 July 2014}}</ref><ref name=Yah>{{cite web|title=World Cup 2014: Goal Control goal-line technology to be used in Brazil|url=https://news.yahoo.com/video/world-cup-2014-goal-control-051750367.html|website=Yahoo! News|accessdate=14 July 2014}}</ref><ref name=TR>{{cite news|last1=WOLDE|first1=HARRO TEN|title=German firm behind goal-line technology looks beyond World Cup|url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/05/29/us-worldcup-technology-idUSKBN0E82EG20140529|website=[[Reuters]]|publisher=[[Thomson Reuters]]|accessdate=14 July 2014|date=29 May 2014}}</ref> France's second goal in their [[2014 FIFA World Cup Group E#France vs Honduras|group game]] against Honduras was the first time goal-line technology was needed to confirm that a goal should be given.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Gibbs|first1=Samuel|title=World Cup goalline technology: how does it work?|url=http://www.theguardian.com/technology/2014/jun/16/world-cup-goalline-technology-football-brazil-2014|publisher=The Guardian|accessdate=18 June 2014}}</ref>

Following successful trials,{{refn|group=nb|The spray was trialled at the [[2013 FIFA U-20 World Cup]], [[2013 FIFA U-17 World Cup]] and [[2013 FIFA Club World Cup]]}} FIFA approved the use of [[vanishing foam]] by the referees for the first time at a World Cup Finals. The water-based spray, which disappears within minutes of application, can be used to mark a ten-yard line for the defending team during a [[Direct free kick|free kick]] and also to draw where the ball is to be placed for a free kick.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://au.eurosport.com/football/world-cup/2014/vanishing-spray-set-for-world-cup_sto4056067/story.shtml|title=Vanishing spray set for World Cup|publisher=eurosport.com|date=21 November 2013}}</ref>

The [[Adidas]] [[Adidas Brazuca|Brazuca]] was the official [[Football (association football)|match ball]] of the 2014 FIFA World Cup<ref name=forbes>{{cite news|last1=Howells|first1=Richard|title=World Cup 2014: Adidas "Brazuca" Ball Scores With Fans|url=http://www.forbes.com/sites/sap/2014/06/11/world-cup-2014-adidas-brazuca-ball-scores-with-fans/|website=Forbes|publisher=Forbes|accessdate=14 June 2014|date=11 June 2014}}</ref><ref name=brazucafifanews>{{cite web|title=adidas Brazuca&nbsp;– Name of Official Match Ball decided by Brazilian fans|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=1693277/|website=FIFA.com |publisher=Fédération Internationale de Football Association |accessdate=3 September 2012}}</ref><ref name=brazucaadidasgroup>{{cite web|title=Brazuca – an icon is born|url=http://www.adidas-group.com/en/group/stories/brazuca-icon-born/|website=Adidas|publisher=Adidas Group|accessdate=14 June 2014}}</ref><ref name=brazucafifamatchball>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/organisation/match-ball.html|title=adidas brings brazuca into the world|date=3 December 2013|website=FIFA.com |publisher=Fédération Internationale de Football Association |accessdate=4 December 2013}}</ref> and was supplied by [[Forward Sports]] of [[Sialkot]], Pakistan.<ref name="forbes"/> Adidas created a new design of ball after criticisms of the [[Adidas Jabulani]] used in the [[2010 FIFA World Cup|previous World Cup]]. The number of panels was reduced to six, with the panels being thermally bonded. This created a ball with increased consistency and aerodynamics compared to its predecessor. Furthermore, Adidas underwent an extensive testing process lasting more than two years to produce a ball that would meet the approval of football professionals.

===Cooling breaks===
Because of the relatively high ambient temperatures in Brazil, particularly at the northern venues, cooling breaks for the players were introduced.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.insideworldfootball.com/fifa/13379-fifa-approves-extra-breaks-to-help-players-keep-their-cool |title=FIFA approves extra breaks to help players keep their cool |publisher=Inside World Football |date=7 October 2013 |accessdate=18 June 2014 }}</ref> Breaks could take place at the referee's discretion after the 30th minute of each half if the [[Wet Bulb Globe Temperature]] exceeded {{convert|32|C}}.

The first cooling break in World Cup play took place during the 32nd minute of the [[2014 FIFA World Cup knockout stage#Netherlands vs Mexico|match between the Netherlands and Mexico in the round of 16]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Heat forces first cooling breaks in Brazil|url=http://www.bbc.com/sport/0/football/28075216|website=BBC Sport|accessdate=2 July 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=A cool first and a historic triumph|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=6/news=a-cool-first-and-a-historic-triumph-2390305.html|website=FIFA.com|accessdate=1 July 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Hicks|first1=Brandon|title=FIFA World Cup: Netherlands 2, Mexico 1|url=http://www.cbc.ca/sports/soccer/brazil2014/post-game/fifa-world-cup-netherlands-2-mexico-1-1.2691278|website=CBC Sports|accessdate=1 July 2014|date=29 June 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=MEX & NED Players take first cooling break|url=https://twitter.com/FIFAWorldCup/status/483287726973014017|website=Twitter}}</ref> At the start of the match, FIFA listed the temperature at {{convert|32|C}} with 68% humidity.<ref>{{cite web|title=Match Report|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/matches/round=255951/match=300186508/index.html#matchfacts|website=FIFA|accessdate=1 July 2014}}</ref>

===Anti-doping===
The [[biological passport]] was introduced in the FIFA World Cup starting in 2014. [[Blood]] and [[urine]] samples from all players before the competition, and from two players per team per match, are analysed by the [[Swiss Laboratory for Doping Analyses]].<ref>{{fr}} [http://fr.m.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=6/news=dvorak-le-profil-biologique-une-approche-completement-nouvelle-2354963.html Anti-dopage. Dvorak : "Le profil biologique, une approche complètement nouvelle"], fifa.com (page visited on 11 June 2014).</ref> FIFA reported that 91.5% of the players taking part in the tournament were tested before the start of the competition and none tested positive.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=6/news=all-pre-fifa-world-cup-doping-controls-test-negative-2374232.html |title=All pre-FIFA World Cup doping controls test negative |publisher=FIFA.com |date=14 June 2014 |accessdate=24 June 2014 }}</ref> However, FIFA was criticised for its approach towards finding doping offences.{{clarify|date=June 2014}}<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.irishtimes.com/sport/other-sports/doping-controls-at-the-world-cup-in-brazil-leave-a-lot-to-be-desired-1.1823913 |title=Doping controls at the World Cup in Brazil leave a lot to be desired – Sports News &#124; Latest Sports Results & Fixtures &#124; The Irish Times – Sat, Jun 07, 2014 |publisher=The Irish Times |date=7 June 2014 |accessdate=24 June 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.telegraph.co.uk/sport/football/world-cup/10917219/World-Cup-2014-Diego-Maradona-accuses-Fifa-of-drug-testing-conspiracy-against-Costa-Rica.html |title=World Cup 2014: Diego Maradona accuses Fifa of drug testing conspiracy against Costa Rica |publisher=Telegraph |date= 21 June 2014|accessdate=24 June 2014 |location=London |first=Ben |last=Rumsby}}</ref>

==Format==
{{anchor|Tie-breaking_criteria}}
The first round, or group stage, was a competition between the 32 teams divided among eight groups of four, where each group engaged in a [[round-robin tournament]] within itself. The two highest ranked teams in each group advanced to the knockout stage.<ref name="regulations"/> Teams were awarded three points for a win and one for a draw. When comparing teams in a group over-all result came before head-to-head.

In the [[single-elimination tournament|knockout stage]] there were four rounds (round of 16, quarter-finals, semi-finals, and the final), with each eliminating the losers. The two semi-final losers competed in a third place play-off. For any match in the knockout stage, a draw after 90 minutes of regulation time was followed by two 15 minute periods of [[Overtime (association football)|extra time]] to determine a winner. If the teams were still tied, a [[Penalty shoot-out (association football)|penalty shoot-out]] was held to determine a winner.<ref name="regulations"/>

The match schedule was announced on 20 October 2011<ref>{{cite web |title=Match schedule for 2014 FIFA World Cup unveiled |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=1529755/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=20 October 2011 }}</ref> with the kick-off times being confirmed on 27 September 2012;<ref>{{cite web |title=FIFA Executive Committee approves kick-off times for Brazil 2014 |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=1709606/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=27 September 2012}}</ref> after the final draw, the kick-off times of seven matches were adjusted by FIFA.<ref>{{cite web |title=Match schedule for Brazil 2014 confirmed |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=2240950/index.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=7 December 2013}}</ref> The competition was organised so that teams that played each other in the group stage could not meet again during the knockout phase until the final (or the 3rd place match).<ref name="regulations"/>
The group stage began on 12 June, with the host nation competing in the opening game as has been the format since the 2006 tournament. The opening game was preceded by an [[2014 FIFA World Cup opening ceremony|opening ceremony]] that began at 15:15 local time.<ref>{{cite web |title=Brazil 2014: Opening ceremony |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/photos/galleries/y=2014/m=6/gallery=brazil-2014-opening-ceremony-2368744.html |publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=12 June 2014}}</ref>

==Match summary==
The 32 teams played a total of 64 matches which included 48 group stage matches, eight round of 16 matches, four quarter-final matches, two semi-finals, a match to decide third place and the [[2014 FIFA World Cup Final|final]] match. Rest days were allocated during the various stages to allow players recovery during the tournament.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/archive/brazil2014/matches/index.html |title=2014 FIFA World Cup Brazil - Matches |publisher=FIFA |accessdate=25 December 2015 }}</ref> All times listed in the table below are in [[Time in Brazil#Brasília official time - BRT (UTC-03)|Brasília official time]] ([[UTC−03:00|UTC−3]]).{{refn|group=nb|This is the time zone of ten of the twelve venues; the other two, [[Cuiabá]] and [[Manaus]], are in the [[Time in Brazil#Brasília time − 1 (UTC-04)|Amazon time zone]] ([[UTC−04:00|UTC−4]]), therefore for matches hosted at these two venues the local kickoff times are one hour earlier than the times listed.<ref>{{cite web |title=MATCH SCHEDULE – 2014 FIFA World Cup Brazil |url=http://resources.fifa.com/mm/document/tournament/competition/01/52/99/91/2014fwc_matchschedule_wgroups_22042014_en_neutral.pdf |format=PDF |publisher=FIFA |accessdate=25 December 2015 }}</ref> The match schedule was announced at FIFA's headquarters in [[Zürich]] on 20 October 2011.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=1529755/index.html |title=Match schedule for 2014 FIFA World Cup unveiled |publisher=FIFA |date=20 October 2011 }}</ref> After the final draw, the kick-off times of seven matches were adjusted by FIFA.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/newsid=2240950/index.html |title=Match schedule for Brazil 2014 confirmed |publisher=FIFA |date=7 December 2013 }}</ref>}}

===Group stage===
{{main|2014 FIFA World Cup group stage}}

The '''group stage of the 2014 FIFA World Cup''' took place in [[Brazil]] from 12 June 2014 to 26 June 2014: each team played three games. The group stage was notable for a scarcity of draws and a large number of goals. The first drawn (and goalless) match did not occur until the 13th match of the tournament, between Iran and Nigeria: a drought longer than any World Cup since 1930.<ref>{{cite news|title=Iran 0–0 Nigeria: Super Eagles play out World Cup's first goalless draw|url=http://www.dailymail.co.uk/sport/worldcup2014/article-2659372/Iran-0-0-Nigeria-Super-Eagles-play-World-Cups-goalless-draw.html | location=London | work=Daily Mail|date=16 June 2014}}</ref> The group stage produced a total of 136 goals (an average of 2.83 goals per match), nine fewer than were scored during the entire 2010 tournament.<ref>{{cite web|title=Group stage goal glut brightens World Cup|url=http://www.dw.de/group-stage-goal-glut-brightens-world-cup/a-17740469|date=26 June 2014|publisher=Deutsche Welle}}</ref> ====Round of 16====
For the first time since the introduction of a round of 16 after the group stage in 1986, all the group winners advanced into the quarter-finals.<ref>{{cite news |title=Belgium complete a World Cup record as all eight group winners make it to the quarter finals for the first time |url=http://www.dailymail.co.uk/sport/worldcup2014/article-2677324/Belgium-complete-World-Cup-record-eight-group-winners-make-quarter-finals-time.html |publisher=[[Daily Mail]] |date=2 July 2014 |accessdate=2 July 2014 |location=London}}</ref> They included four teams from UEFA, three from CONMEBOL, and one from CONCACAF. Of the eight matches, five required extra-time, and two of these [[List of FIFA World Cup penalty shoot-outs|required penalty shoot-outs]]; this was the first time penalty shoot-outs happened in more than one game in a round of 16.{{refn|group=nb|In [[1938 FIFA World Cup#First round|1938's round of 16]], two games were also tied after extra-time, but those were [[Replay (sports)|replay]]ed instead.}} The goal average per game in the round of 16 was 2.25, a drop of 0.58 goals per game from the group stage.<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.cbc.ca/sports/soccer/brazil2014/world-cup-round-of-16-by-the-numbers-1.2693626 | work=CBC News | first=Brandon | last=Hicks | title=World Cup Round of 16: By the numbers | date=1 July 2014}}</ref> The eight teams to win in the round of 16 included four former champions (Brazil, Germany, Argentina and France), a three-time runner-up (Netherlands), and two first-time quarter-finalists (Colombia and Costa Rica).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.soccerbyives.net/2014/06/colombia-uruguay-quarterfinals.html|title=Colombia topples Uruguay to reach first World Cup quarterfinals|access-date=2015-09-20|date=2014-06-28|first1=Carl|last1=Setterlund}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://time.com/2938804/costa-rica-beats-greece-5-3-for-quarterfinal-clash-with-dutch/ |title=Costa Rica Beats Greece 5-3 to Advance to Their First Ever World Cup Quarterfinal |date=29 June 2014 |first=John |last=Pye |publisher=TIME |deadurl=yes |archive-url=https://web.https://www.web.archive.org/web/20140708061130/http://time.com/2938804/costa-rica-beats-greece-5-3-for-quarterfinal-clash-with-dutch/ |archive-date=8 July 2014 }} </ref> Belgium reached the quarter-finals for the first time since 1986.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://sports.ndtv.com/fifa-world-cup-2014/news/226325-world-cup-2014-belgium-vs-usa|title=World Cup 2014: Belgium Beat USA to Set Up Quarterfinal Clash vs Argentina|author=Agence France-Presse|date=1 July 2014|work=NDTVSports.com}}</ref>

====Quarter-finals====
With a 1–0 victory over France, Germany set a World Cup record with four consecutive semi-final appearances. Brazil beat Colombia 2–1, but Brazil's [[Neymar]] was injured and missed the rest of the competition. Argentina reached the final four for the first time since 1990 after a 1–0 win over Belgium. The Netherlands reached the semi-finals for the second consecutive tournament, after overcoming Costa Rica in a [[Penalty shootout (football)|penalty shoot-out]] following a 0–0 draw at the end of extra time.

====Semi-finals====
Germany qualified for the final for the eighth time with a [[Brazil v Germany (2014 FIFA World Cup)|7–1 win over Brazil]] – the biggest defeat in Brazilian history since 1920. [[Miroslav Klose]]'s goal in this match was his 16th throughout all World Cups, breaking the record he had previously shared with [[Ronaldo]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://abcnews.go.com/Sports/wireStory/klose-scores-brazil-set-world-cup-record-24474582|title=Sports News|author=ABC News|work=ABC News}}</ref> Klose set another record by becoming the first player to appear in four World Cup semi-finals.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.leaderpost.com/sports/Klose+travels+anomalous+path+become+time+World+scorer/10017042/story.html|archiveurl=http://web.archive.org/web/20141015014509/http://www.leaderpost.com/sports/Klose+travels+anomalous+path+become+time+World+scorer/10017042/story.html|title=Klose travels anomalous path to become all-time top World Cup scorer|archivedate=15 October 2014|work=www.leaderpost.com}}</ref> Argentina reached their first final since 1990, and the fifth overall after overcoming the Netherlands in a [[Penalty shoot-out (association football)|penalty shoot-out]] following a 0–0 draw at the end of extra time.

This marked the first time that teams from the same continent had won three consecutive World Cups (following Italy in [[2006 FIFA World Cup|2006]] and Spain in [[2010 FIFA World Cup|2010]]). It was also the first time that a European nation had won the World Cup in the Americas. On aggregate Europe now has 11 victories, compared to South America's 9 victories.

===Prize money===
The total prize money on offer for the tournament was confirmed by FIFA as [[United States dollar|US]]$576 million (including payments of $70 million to domestic clubs and $100 million as player insurances), a 37 percent increase from the amount [[2010 FIFA World Cup#Prize money|allocated in the 2010 tournament]]. Before the tournament, each of the 32 entrants received $1.5 million for preparation costs. At the tournament, the prize money was distributed as follows:<ref name=autogenerated1>{{cite news|title=World Cup money pot increased to $576m|url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2013/12/05/us-soccer-world-prizemoney-idUSBRE9B40QG20131205|publisher=reuters.com|accessdate=25 April 2014|date=5 December 2013}}</ref>

* $8 million – To each team eliminated at the [[#Group stage|group stage]] (16 teams)
* $9 million – To each team eliminated in the [[#Round of 16|round of 16]] (8 teams)
* $14 million – To each team eliminated in the [[#Quarter-finals|quarter-finals]] (4 teams)
* $20 million – [[#Third_place_play-off|Fourth placed team]]
* $22 million – [[#Third_place_play-off|Third placed team]]
* $25 million – [[#Final|Runner-up]]
* $35 million – [[#Final|Winner]]

==Preparations and costs==
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup preparations}}
Forecasts on the eve of the tournament estimated that the cost to the Brazilian government would be US$14&nbsp;billion, making it the most expensive World Cup to date.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.espnfc.com/news/story/_/id/1830732/2014-world-cup-set-most-lucrative-ever?cc=5739|title=World Cup set to be most lucrative ever|date=23 May 2014|publisher=ESPN}}</ref> FIFA is expected to spend US$2&nbsp;billion on staging the finals,<ref>{{cite news|title=Record World Cup numbers game for FIFA, Brazil|url=http://www.usatoday.com/story/money/business/2014/05/22/record-world-cup-numbers-game-for-fifa-and-brazil/9458457/|publisher=USA Today|date=22 May 2014|first=Graham|last=Dunbar}}</ref> with its greatest single expense being the [[#Prize money|US$576&nbsp;million prize money pot]].<ref name=autogenerated1 />

Although organisers originally estimated costs of US$1.1&nbsp;billion,<ref name="Inspection report">{{cite web |title=FIFA's Inspection Report |url=http://www.fifa.com/mm/document/affederation/mission/fwc2014%5fbrazil%5fbid%5finspection%5freport%5fen%5f24491.pdf |format=PDF |accessdate=9 October 2011 }}</ref> a reported US$3.6&nbsp;billion was ultimately spent on stadium works.<ref>{{cite web |title=Corruption to blame for some Brazil World Cup cost rises |url=http://www.businessweek.com/news/2014-05-22/corruption-to-blame-for-some-brazil-world-cup-cost-rises |publisher=Bloomberg |date=23 May 2014 }}</ref><ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2012/04/03/soccer-world-brazil-idUSL2E8F2GG820120403 |title=Brazil World Cup stadiums on track, but costs soar |publisher=Reuters |date=3 April 2012 }}</ref> Five of the chosen host cities had brand new venues built specifically for the World Cup, while the [[Estádio Nacional Mané Garrincha]] in the capital [[Brasília]] was demolished and rebuilt, with the remaining six being extensively renovated.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/sport/0/football/24897388 |title=2014 Fifa World Cup: Where are the 12 host stadiums in Brazil? |publisher=BBC Sport (British Broadcasting Corporation) |date=3 December 2013}}</ref>

An additional R$3&nbsp;billion (US$1.3&nbsp;billion, €960&nbsp;million, £780&nbsp;million at June 2014 rates) was earmarked by the Brazilian government for investment in infrastructure works and projects for use during the 2014 World Cup and beyond.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www2.anba.com.br/noticia_esportes.kmf?cod=8705153|title=FIFA World Cup 2014 host cities to receive US$ 1.5 billion|date=22 July 2009|publisher=Brazil-Arab News Agency}}</ref> However, the failed completion of many of the proposed works provoked discontent among some Brazilians.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/04/08/us-worldcup-brazil-idUSBREA3715H20140408|title=Less than half of Brazilians favor hosting World Cup, poll shows|publisher=Reuters|date=8 April 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://bleacherreport.com/articles/1663701-the-social-cost-of-brazil-hosting-world-cup-2014|title=The social cost of Brazil hosting World Cup 2014|publisher=Bleacher Report|date=6 June 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Fifa concern about three Brazil stadiums |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/sport/0/football/27632572 |publisher=BBC Sport (British Broadcasting Corporation) |date=30 May 2014}}</ref>

The Brazilian government pledged US$900&nbsp;million to be invested into security forces and that the tournament would be "one of the most protected sports events in history."<ref name="CNN">{{cite news|url=http://edition.cnn.com/2012/12/05/sport/football/brazil-world-cup-security-football/|title=Brazil boosts World Cup security budget as crime rises|publisher=CNN|date=5 December 2013}}</ref>

==Marketing==
[[File:FulecoMascot.jpg|thumb|[[Fuleco]], the official mascot of the 2014 FIFA World Cup]]
{{Main|2014 FIFA World Cup marketing}}
The marketing of the 2014 FIFA World Cup included sale of tickets, support from sponsors and promotion through events that utilise the symbols and [[FIFA World Cup official songs|songs]] of the tournament. Popular merchandise included items featuring the [[Fuleco|official mascot]] as well as an [[2014 FIFA World Cup Brazil (video game)|official video game]] that has been developed by [[EA Sports]].<ref name="videogame1">{{cite web|url=http://www.ign.com/articles/2014/02/06/ea-announces-ea-sports-2014-fifa-world-cup-brazil |title=EA Announces EA SPORTS 2014 FIFA World Cup Brazil |publisher=IGN |date=6 February 2014 |accessdate=24 February 2014}}</ref> The official song of the tournament was "[[We Are One (Ole Ola)]]" with vocals from [[Pitbull (rapper)|Pitbull]], [[Jennifer Lopez]] and [[Claudia Leitte]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=1/news=brazilian-star-claudia-leitte-perform-with-pitbull-and-jennifer-lopez-th-2264459.html|title=Brazilian star Claudia Leitte to perform with Pitbull and Jennifer Lopez on the official song for the 2014 FIFA World Cup|date=23 January 2014|publisher=FIFA}}</ref> As a partner of the [[German Football Association]], the German airline [[Lufthansa]] renamed itself "Fanhansa" on some of its planes that flew the German national team, media representatives and football fans to Brazil.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Drum|first1=Bruce|title=Lufthansa to rename 8 aircraft "Fanhansa" for the 2014 FIFA World Cup|url=http://worldairlinenews.com/2014/05/13/lufthansa-to-renamed-8-aircraft-fanhansa-for-the-2014-fifa-world-cup/|accessdate=15 July 2014}}</ref>

==Media==
{{See also|2014 FIFA World Cup broadcasting rights}}
For a fourth consecutive FIFA World Cup Finals, the coverage was provided by HBS (Host Broadcast Services), a subsidiary of [[Infront Sports & Media]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/aboutfifa/organisation/tv/hostbroadcasting.html|title=Host Broadcasting|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |accessdate=4 December 2013}}</ref> [[Sony]] was selected as the official equipment provider and built 12 bespoke [[High-definition video|high definition]] production 40-foot-long containers, one for each tournament venue, to house the extensive amount of equipment required.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.live-production.tv/news/sports/sony-professional-awarded-2014-fifa-world-cup%E2%84%A2-broadcast-production-contract.html |title=Sony Professional awarded 2014 FIFA World Cup Each match utilised 37 standard camera plans, including Aerial and Cablecam, two Ultramotion cameras and dedicated cameras for interviews.<ref name="redsharknews1"/> [[List of official FIFA World Cup films|The official tournament film]], as well as three matches,{{refn|group=nb|Those matches scheduled to be filmed in ultra high definition were one match from the round of 16 (on 28 June), one Confederations Cup]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=4/news=sony-and-fifa-announce-further-coverage-the-2014-fifa-world-cuptm-2313778.html|title=Sony and FIFA announce further 4K coverage of the 2014 FIFA World Cup|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=3 April 2014}}</ref>

The broadcasting rights&nbsp;– covering television, radio, internet and mobile coverage&nbsp;– for the tournament were sold to media companies in each individual territory either directly by FIFA, or through licensed companies or organisations such as the [[European Broadcasting Union]], [[Organización de Televisión Iberoamericana]], International Media Content, [[Dentsu]] and RS International Broadcasting & Sports Management.<ref>{{cite news|title=2014 FIFA World Cup Brazil<sup>TM</sup> Media Rights Licensees|url=http://www.fifa.com/mm/document/affederation/tv/01/47/76/00/2014fifaworldcupbrazil%28tm%29mediarightslicenseelist110414_neutral.pdf|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=7 November 2013}}</ref> The sale of these rights accounted for an estimated 60% of FIFA's income from staging a World Cup.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.conmebol.com/en/content/fifa-revenue-estimated-be-4-billion-dollars-close-2014-world-cup|title=FIFA revenue estimated to be 4 billion dollars at the close of the 2014 World Cup|publisher=CONMEBOL.com|date=17 May 2013}}</ref> The International Broadcast Centre was situated at the [[Riocentro]] in the [[Barra da Tijuca, Rio de Janeiro|Barra da Tijuca]] neighbourhood of Rio de Janeiro.<ref>{{cite news|title=International Broadcast Centre to be hosted in Rio de Janeiro|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/media/newsid=1442394/|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=27 May 2011}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=Nerve centre for World Cup TV production opens in Rio|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2014/m=6/news=nerve-centre-for-world-cup-tv-production-opens-in-rio-2353342.html|publisher=FIFA.com (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) |date=2 June 2014}}</ref>

Worldwide, several games qualified as the most-watched sporting events in their country in 2014, including 42.9 million people in Brazil for the opening game between Brazil and Croatia, the 34.1 million in Japan who saw their team play Ivory Coast, and 34.7 million in Germany who saw their national team win the World Cup against Argentina,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.focus.de/sport/fussball/wm-2014/wm-die-top-10-quoten-aller-zeiten-im-deutschen-fernsehen_id_3989104.html |title=WM: Die Top-10-Quoten aller Zeiten im deutschen Fernsehen |language=de |publisher=Focus Online |date=14 July 2014 |accessdate=14 July 2014 }}</ref> while the 24.7 million viewers during the game between the USA and Portugal is joint with the 2010 final as the most-watched football game in the United States.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://latino.foxnews.com/latino/money/2014/06/24/usa-vs-portugal-world-cup-match-most-watched-soccer-game-in-us-history/ |title=USA vs. Portugal World Cup Match Most Watched Soccer Game In U.S. History |publisher=Fox News |date= 24 June 2014|accessdate=27 June 2014 }}</ref> According to FIFA, over one billion people tuned in worldwide to watch [[2014 FIFA World Cup Final|the final]] between Germany and Argentina.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.fifa.com/worldcup/news/y=2015/m=12/news=2014-fifa-world-cuptm-reached-3-2-billion-viewers-one-billion-watched--2745519.html|title=2014 FIFA World Cup™ reached 3.2 billion viewers, one billion watched final|date=16 December 2015|work=FIFA.com}}</ref>

==Controversies==
{{main|List of 2014 FIFA World Cup controversies}}
<!-- See Talk:2014_FIFA_World_Cup#RFC or https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Talk:2014_FIFA_World_Cup&oldid=614777115#RFC -->
The 2014 FIFA World Cup generated various [[Controversy|controversies]], including demonstrations, some of which took place even before the tournament started. Furthermore, there were various issues with [[safety]], including eight deaths of workers and a fire during construction, breaches into stadiums, an unstable makeshift staircase at the [[Maracanã Stadium]], a [[monorail]] collapse, and the [[Belo Horizonte overpass collapse|collapse of an unfinished overpass]] in [[Belo Horizonte]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Factbox: Timeline of deaths, accidents at Brazil's World Cup stadiums|url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/02/15/us-brazil-worldcup-stadiums-factbox-idUSBREA1E11S20140215|website=http://www.reuters.com/|publisher=[[Reuters]]|accessdate=29 June 2014|date=15 February 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Brazil World Cup: seventh worker dies on stadium construction|url=http://www.theguardian.com/football/2014/mar/30/brazil-world-cup-worker-dies|website=theguardian.com/us|publisher=[[Associated Press]]|accessdate=29 June 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|title=Brazil World Cup 2014: Eighth death at football stadiums|url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-latin-america-27337339|website=www.bbc.com|publisher=[[BBC News]]|accessdate=29 June 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=Tony Jimenez|first1=Andrew|title=Soccer-Cuiaba fire adds to Brazil's World Cup woes|url=https://sports.yahoo.com/news/soccer-cuiaba-fire-adds-brazils-world-cup-woes-205257620--sow.html|website=https://sports.yahoo.com/|publisher=[[Reuters]]|accessdate=29 June 2014}}</ref><ref name="Monorail collapses BBC">{{cite news|title=Fatal accident on flagship Sao Paulo monorail|url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-latin-america-27774337|work=[[BBC News]]|date=9 June 2014|accessdate=5 July 2014|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140704090344/http://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-27774337|archivedate=4 July 2014}}</ref> The houses of thousands of families in [[Rio de Janeiro]] were cleared for redevelopments for the World Cup in spite of protests and resistance. Favela do Metrô, near the Maracanã Stadium, was completely destroyed as a result, having previously housed 700 families in 2010.<ref>Gibson, Owen & Watts, Jonathan (5 December 2013). [http://www.theguardian.com/world/2013/dec/05/world-cup-favelas-socially-cleansed-olympics World Cup: Rio favelas being 'socially cleansed' in runup to sporting events]. ''Guardian''. Retrieved on 3 March 2015.</ref><ref>Phillips, Tom (26 April 2011). [http://www.theguardian.com/world/2011/apr/26/favela-ghost-town-rio-world-cup Rio World Cup demolitions leave favela families trapped in ghost town]. ''The Guardian''. Retrieved on 3 March 2015.</ref><ref>Hodges, Elena (21 June 2014). [http://www.rioonwatch.org/?p=16094 The World Cup Is Underway. What Has Become of Favela do Metrô?]. Rio On Watch. Retrieved on 3 March 2015.</ref>

In the tournament itself, controversy centred around [[2014 FIFA World Cup officials|officiating]], with international [[Referee (association football)|referees]] including [[Yuichi Nishimura]], [[Milorad Mažić]], [[Enrique Osses]], [[Peter O'Leary (referee)|Peter O'Leary]], [[Ravshan Irmatov]], [[Howard Webb]], [[Mark Geiger]], [[Carlos Velasco Carballo]], and assistant Humberto Clavijo coming under criticism for their performances. The most notable disciplinary case was that of Uruguayan striker [[Luis Suárez]], who was disciplined after [[List of 2014 FIFA World Cup controversies#Uruguay vs Italy|biting Italian player]] [[Giorgio Chiellini]] during the game between [[Italy national football team|Italy]] and [[Uruguay national football team|Uruguay]].

===Breaches into stadiums===
{{further|List of 2014 FIFA World Cup controversies#Breaches into stadiums}}
At the [[2014 FIFA World Cup Group B#Spain vs Chile|Group B match]] between [[Spain national football team|Spain]] and [[Chile national football team|Chile]], around 100 Chilean supporters who had gathered outside [[Maracanã Stadium]] forced their way into the stadium and caused damage to the media centre. Military police reported that 85 Chileans were detained during the events, while others reached the stands. Earlier, about 20 Argentinians made a similar breach during Argentina's [[2014 FIFA World Cup Group F#Argentina vs Bosnia and Herzegovina|Group F game]] against Bosnia and Herzegovina at the same stadium.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.theguardian.com/football/2014/jun/18/world-cup-2014-chile-fans-maracana-spain | last=Wilson | first=Jonathan | title=World Cup 2014: Chile fans invade Maracanã before Spain game. | newspaper=The Guardian | date =18 June 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2014-06-18/world-cup-chile-fans-storm-rio-stadium-ahead-of-spain-game.html|title=World Cup Chile Fans Storm Rio Stadium And 85 Detained | work=Bloomberg | date=19 June 2014}}</ref>

===Bridge collapse===
{{main|Belo Horizonte overpass collapse}}
On 3 July 2014, an [[overpass]] under construction in [[Belo Horizonte]] as part of the World Cup [[infrastructure]] projects collapsed onto a busy [[carriageway]] below, leaving two people dead and 22 others injured.<ref name="Overpass collapses BBC">{{cite news |url=http://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-28155216 |title=Flyover collapses in Brazil World Cup host city |work=[[BBC News]] |date=4 July 2014 |accessdate=4 July 2014 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140705021338/http://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-28155216 |archivedate=5 July 2014}}</ref><ref name="Overpass collapses Reuters">{{cite news |title=Overpass collapses in World Cup city, crushes vehicles |agency=[[Reuters]] |date=3 July 2014 |url=http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/07/03/us-brazil-worldcup-accident-idUSKBN0F82A220140703 |last=Cawthorne |first=Andrew |accessdate=4 July 2014 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140705040415/http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/07/03/us-brazil-worldcup-accident-idUSKBN0F82A220140703 |archivedate=5 July 2014}}</ref>

===Head injuries===
During the tournament, FIFA received significant criticism for the way head injuries are handled during matches. Two incidents in particular attracted the most attention. First, in a group stage match, after Uruguayan defender [[Álvaro Pereira]] received a blow to the head, he lay unconscious.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.smh.com.au/fifa-world-cup-2014/world-cup-news-2014/alvaro-pereira-knocked-out-then-raring-to-go-20140620-zsfqt.html|title=Alvaro Pereira, knocked out then raring to go|author=Tom Decent|work=The Sydney Morning Herald}}</ref> The Uruguayan doctor signaled for the player to be substituted, but he returned to the match. The incident drew criticism from the professional players' union FIFPro, and from Michel D'Hooghe, a member of the FIFA executive board and chairman of its medical committee.<ref>[http://www.si.com/soccer/2014/07/13/ap-soc-wcup-final-concussion "Kramer head injury revives concussion concern"], Sports Illustrated, 13 July 2014.</ref>

Second, in the Final, German midfielder [[Christoph Kramer]] received a blow to the head from a collision in the 14th minute, but returned to the match before collapsing in the 31st minute. During that time, Kramer was disoriented and confused, and asked the referee [[Nicola Rizzoli]] whether the match he was playing in was the World Cup Final.<ref>[http://www.si.com/planet-futbol/2014/07/17/christoph-kramer-germany-didnt-know-world-cup-final-referee "Referee: Christoph Kramer unsure it was World Cup final after head blow"], Sports Illustrated, 17 July 2014.</ref>
